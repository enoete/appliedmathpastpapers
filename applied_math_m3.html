SUVAT — Equations of Uniform Acceleration

$v = u + at$
$s = \tfrac{1}{2}(u+v)t$
$s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$
$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$
$s = vt - \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$

Calculus Relationships

$v = \dfrac{dx}{dt} = \dot{x}$
$a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} = v\dfrac{dv}{dx} = \ddot{x}$

Velocity–Time Graph Areas

Area under $v$-$t$ graph $=$ displacement
Gradient of $v$-$t$ graph $=$ acceleration

Resolving Initial Velocity (angle $\theta$, speed $V$)

Horizontal: $u_x = V\cos\theta$ (constant)
Vertical: $u_y = V\sin\theta$

Position at Time $t$

$x = Vt\cos\theta$
$y = Vt\sin\theta - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2$

Key Quantities

Time of flight: $T = \dfrac{2V\sin\theta}{g}$
Greatest height: $H = \dfrac{V^2\sin^2\theta}{2g}$
Horizontal range: $R = \dfrac{V^2\sin2\theta}{g}$
Maximum range $= \dfrac{V^2}{g}$ (when $\theta=45°$)

Trajectory Equation

$y = x\tan\theta - \dfrac{gx^2}{2V^2\cos^2\theta} = x\tan\theta - \dfrac{gx^2}{2V^2}(1+\tan^2\theta)$

Newton's Laws

1st: Body remains at rest or uniform velocity unless acted on by a net force
2nd: $F = ma$ (net force = mass × acceleration)
3rd: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction

Friction

$F \leq \mu R$ (limiting: $F = \mu R$)
$\mu$ = coefficient of friction
On inclined plane: $R = mg\cos\theta$, $F = \mu mg\cos\theta$

Resolving Forces

Equilibrium: $\sum F_x = 0$, $\sum F_y = 0$
Incline (up +ve): Along plane: $F - mg\sin\theta - \text{friction} = ma$
Perpendicular to incline: $R = mg\cos\theta$

Lami's Theorem (3 concurrent forces in equilibrium)

$\dfrac{F_1}{\sin\alpha} = \dfrac{F_2}{\sin\beta} = \dfrac{F_3}{\sin\gamma}$ where $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ are angles opposite to $F_1,F_2,F_3$

Moments (Rod / Beam problems)

Moment = Force × perpendicular distance
Equilibrium: $\sum$ Moments about any point $= 0$

Work

$W = Fd\cos\theta$ (constant force)
Work done against gravity $= mgh$

Energy

Kinetic Energy: $KE = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$
Gravitational PE: $GPE = mgh$
Work-Energy Theorem: $W_{net} = \Delta KE$
Conservation of Energy: $KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2$ (no friction)
With friction: $KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2 + W_{\text{friction}}$

Power

$P = Fv$ (force × velocity)
$P = \dfrac{\text{Work done}}{\text{time}}$
1 Watt = 1 J/s; 1 kW = 1000 W

Momentum

Momentum $p = mv$ (kg m s$^{-1}$)
Conservation: $m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2$
Perfectly inelastic (fuse): $(m_1+m_2)v = m_1u_1 + m_2u_2$

Impulse

Impulse $J = Ft = \Delta p = mv - mu$
Unit: N·s or kg m s$^{-1}$
For wall bounce (take +ve direction): $J = m(v-u)$ — take care with signs!

F1
Three coplanar forces of magnitude 500 N, 750 N and 1200 N act at a point. The 500 N force acts vertically downward, the 750 N force acts horizontally to the right, and the 1200 N force acts at 40° above the horizontal (to the upper left).
  1. List Newton's laws of motion.
  2. Determine the magnitude of the resultant $R$.
  3. Determine the direction of $R$: find angle $\alpha$ to the horizontal.
💡 Hints
Hint 1 — Newton's Laws
Three laws: (1) Inertia, (2) $F=ma$, (3) Action-reaction. Write them out in full.
Hint 2 — Resolve horizontally and vertically
Resolve each force into $x$ (horizontal) and $y$ (vertical) components. Sum them: $R_x = \sum F_x$, $R_y = \sum F_y$.
Hint 3 — Resultant magnitude & direction
$R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}$, $\alpha = \tan^{-1}\!\left(\dfrac{|R_y|}{|R_x|}\right)$.
📋 Full Steps
Part (i) — Newton's Laws
1st: A body remains at rest or moves with uniform velocity unless acted on by a resultant force.
2nd: The resultant force on a body equals its mass times its acceleration ($F=ma$).
3rd: For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction.
Part (ii) — Resolve components (take right = +x, up = +y)
500 N (down): $F_x=0$, $F_y=-500$
750 N (right): $F_x=+750$, $F_y=0$
1200 N (upper-left at 40° to horizontal): $F_x=-1200\cos40°=-919.3$, $F_y=+1200\sin40°=+771.3$
Sum components
$R_x = 750 - 919.3 = -169.3$ N
$R_y = -500 + 771.3 = +271.3$ N
Magnitude
$R = \sqrt{(-169.3)^2+(271.3)^2} = \sqrt{28662+73584} = \sqrt{102246} \approx 319.8$ N
Part (iii) — Direction
$\tan\alpha = \dfrac{|R_y|}{|R_x|} = \dfrac{271.3}{169.3} = 1.602$
$\alpha = \tan^{-1}(1.602) \approx 58.0°$
Since $R_x < 0$ and $R_y > 0$: resultant is in the 2nd quadrant, i.e. upper-left, at $58°$ above the horizontal (or $180°-58°=122°$ from +x-axis).
✔ Answer
(ii) $R \approx \mathbf{319.8}$ N
(iii) $\alpha \approx \mathbf{58.0°}$ above horizontal, directed upper-left (2nd quadrant)
F2
A uniform rod, PQ, weight 40 N, is hinged at P and held horizontal by a string from Q to R, which is vertically above the hinge. The string is inclined at 45° to the rod. Let $T$ = tension in the string.
  1. Draw the coplanar force diagram.
  2. Calculate the tension $T$ in the string.
💡 Hints
Forces on rod
Three forces: hinge reaction at P (unknown), weight 40 N downward at midpoint (uniform rod), tension $T$ at Q at 45° to rod.
Taking moments about P
Taking moments about P eliminates the hinge reaction. Moment of $T$ about P = $T\sin45° \times L$. Moment of weight = $40 \times \frac{L}{2}$. Set equal.
📋 Full Steps
Setup
Let rod PQ have length $L$. Weight acts at midpoint ($L/2$ from P). String at Q makes 45° with rod, so upward component of tension = $T\sin45°$.
Moments about P (clockwise +ve)
Clockwise: weight $= 40 \times \dfrac{L}{2} = 20L$
Anticlockwise: vertical component of $T$ at Q $= T\sin45° \times L$
Equilibrium: $T\sin45° \times L = 20L$
Solve
$T\sin45° = 20$
$T \times \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = 20$
$T = \dfrac{20\sqrt{2}}{1} = 20\sqrt{2} \approx 28.28$ N
✔ Answer
$T = 20\sqrt{2} \approx \mathbf{28.3}$ N
F3
A ring X is on a fixed pole AB inclined at 40° to the horizontal. A string over a smooth pulley P is attached to the ring at one end and to an object Y of mass 15 kg at the other. The coefficient of friction between ring and pole is 0.3; the ring is just about to slip up the pole.
  1. Label the diagram showing forces on the ring.
  2. State one condition for the ring to be in equilibrium.
  3. Determine the mass of the ring.
  4. Determine the normal reaction of the rod on the ring.
💡 Hints
Forces on ring
4 forces: weight $mg$ downward, tension $T=15g$ along string (horizontal, toward pulley P), normal reaction $N$ perpendicular to pole, friction $F$ along pole (down the pole, since about to slip UP).
Limiting friction
Since about to slip: $F = \mu N = 0.3N$, directed down the slope.
Resolve parallel and perpendicular to pole
Tension acts horizontally. Resolve into components along and perpendicular to the inclined pole.
📋 Full Steps
Setup
$T = 15g = 15 \times 9.8 = 147$ N (horizontal, as string goes to pulley P which is horizontal from ring).
Pole inclined at 40° to horizontal. About to slip UP → friction acts DOWN the pole.
Resolve perpendicular to pole (outward +ve)
$N = T\sin40° + mg\sin50°$
Wait — let's use a cleaner approach. Take x-axis along pole (up +ve), y-axis perpendicular to pole (away from pole +ve).
Tension horizontal: component along pole $= T\cos40°$ (up the pole), component perpendicular $= T\sin40°$ (away from pole).
Perpendicular to pole
$N = T\sin40° + mg\cos40°$
But wait: weight $mg$ acts downward. Component of weight perpendicular to pole (into pole) $= mg\cos(90°-40°) = mg\sin40°$... Let's be careful:
Pole at 40° to horizontal. Weight is vertical. Component of weight perpendicular to pole (towards pole) $= mg\sin40°$ (since angle between weight and the perpendicular-to-pole direction is $90°-40°=50°$... actually: angle between weight vertical and pole direction is $90°-40°=50°$, so perp component $= mg\sin(50°) = mg\cos40°$).
Simpler: Perpendicular to pole: $N - T\sin40° - mg\cos40° = 0$, so $N = T\sin40° + mg\cos40°$.
Along the pole (up +ve)
$T\cos40° - mg\sin40° - F = 0$
Limiting friction: $F = 0.3N$
$T\cos40° - mg\sin40° - 0.3(T\sin40° + mg\cos40°) = 0$
Substitute $T=147$ N
$147\cos40° - mg\sin40° - 0.3(147\sin40° + mg\cos40°) = 0$
$147(0.766) - 0.3(147)(0.643) - mg\sin40° - 0.3mg\cos40° = 0$
$112.6 - 28.4 - mg(0.643 + 0.3\times0.766) = 0$
$84.2 = mg(0.643 + 0.230) = mg(0.873)$
$mg = \dfrac{84.2}{0.873} = 96.4$ N
$m = \dfrac{96.4}{9.8} \approx 9.84$ kg
Normal reaction
$N = T\sin40° + mg\cos40° = 147(0.643) + 96.4(0.766) = 94.5 + 73.9 = 168.4$ N
✔ Answer
(ii) Condition: net force on ring = 0 (vector sum of all forces is zero)
(iii) Mass of ring $\approx \mathbf{9.84}$ kg
(iv) Normal reaction $N \approx \mathbf{168.4}$ N
F4
A clock weighing 40 N is suspended from a ceiling by a light inextensible string (vertical). A horizontal force of 15 N acts on the clock. A second force $T$ at angle $\theta$ to the vertical holds the system in place with the first string remaining vertical.
  1. Using Lami's Theorem, formulate an equation in terms of $T$ and $\theta$.
  2. Determine the value of $\theta$.
  3. Calculate the value of $T$.
💡 Hints
Three concurrent forces
Three forces on clock: 40 N down, 15 N right, $T$ at angle $\theta$ to vertical (upper-left direction). Since in equilibrium, resolve horizontally and vertically.
Lami's Theorem
$\dfrac{F_1}{\sin\alpha_1} = \dfrac{F_2}{\sin\alpha_2} = \dfrac{F_3}{\sin\alpha_3}$ where each $\alpha$ is the angle opposite (i.e., between) the other two forces.
Alternative: simple resolve
Horizontal: $T\sin\theta = 15$. Vertical: $T\cos\theta = 40$. Divide to find $\theta$.
📋 Full Steps
Part (i) — Lami's Theorem setup
The three forces are: $W=40$ N (down), $H=15$ N (right), $T$ (upper-left at $\theta$ to vertical).
Angles between forces: angle between $W$ and $H$ = 90°, so angle opposite $T$ = $90°+\theta$... More carefully:
Angle between $W$ (down) and $H$ (right) = 90°, so opposite angle for $T$ = $360°-90°-(90°+\theta)$... Lami's works with angles between adjacent force vectors measured going around. The angle between W and T = $180°-\theta$; angle between T and H = $90°+\theta$; angle between H and W = $90°$... This can get complex.
Simplest approach: Resolve directly.
Parts (ii) & (iii) — Resolve
$T$ acts upper-left at angle $\theta$ to vertical.
Horizontal equilibrium: $T\sin\theta = 15$ ...(1)
Vertical equilibrium: $T\cos\theta = 40$ ...(2)
Divide (1) by (2)
$\tan\theta = \dfrac{15}{40} = 0.375$
$\theta = \tan^{-1}(0.375) = 20.6°$
Find T
From (2): $T = \dfrac{40}{\cos20.6°} = \dfrac{40}{0.9354} = 42.8$ N
Or: $T = \sqrt{15^2 + 40^2} = \sqrt{225+1600} = \sqrt{1825} \approx 42.7$ N
✔ Answer
(ii) $\theta = \tan^{-1}(0.375) \approx \mathbf{20.6°}$
(iii) $T = \sqrt{1825} \approx \mathbf{42.7}$ N
F5
An object of mass 5 kg is suspended by two strings from a ceiling. String $T_1$ makes 60° to the vertical; string $T_2$ makes 30° to the vertical. A third string $T$ pulls the mass straight down. Given that the system is in equilibrium, determine the tensions $T$, $T_1$ and $T_2$.
💡 Hints
Key insight
There are two junction points. At the mass: $T = mg = 5 \times 9.8 = 49$ N. At the upper junction: three forces ($T_1$, $T_2$, $T$) — resolve horizontally and vertically.
At upper junction
$T_1\sin60° = T_2\sin30°$ (horizontal), $T_1\cos60° + T_2\cos30° = T$ (vertical).
📋 Full Steps
Step 1 — Find T
At the mass, $T$ balances weight: $T = 5g = 5 \times 9.8 = 49$ N
Step 2 — At upper junction, horizontal
$T_1\sin60° = T_2\sin30°$
$T_1\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = T_2\dfrac{1}{2}$
$T_1\sqrt{3} = T_2$ → $T_2 = \sqrt{3}\,T_1$ ...(1)
Step 3 — At upper junction, vertical
$T_1\cos60° + T_2\cos30° = T = 49$
$T_1\cdot\dfrac{1}{2} + \sqrt{3}\,T_1\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 49$
$\dfrac{T_1}{2} + \dfrac{3T_1}{2} = 49$
$2T_1 = 49 \Rightarrow T_1 = 24.5$ N
Step 4 — Find $T_2$
$T_2 = \sqrt{3}\times24.5 = 24.5\sqrt{3} \approx 42.4$ N
✔ Answer
$T = 49$ N
$T_1 = 24.5$ N
$T_2 = 24.5\sqrt{3} \approx 42.4$ N
F6
A block of mass 8 kg is pulled upwards along a rough plane inclined at 30° by a force of 95 N. The coefficient of friction between block and plane is 0.4.
  1. Draw a fully labelled diagram showing all forces on the block.
  2. Calculate the acceleration of the block.
💡 Hints
Four forces
Applied force 95 N (up the slope), weight $mg$ (vertically down), normal reaction $R$ (perpendicular to slope), friction $F$ (down the slope — opposing motion).
Resolve perpendicular to slope
$R = mg\cos30°$. Then $F = \mu R = 0.4mg\cos30°$.
Resolve along slope (up +ve)
$95 - mg\sin30° - F = ma$.
📋 Full Steps
Normal reaction
$R = mg\cos30° = 8\times9.8\times\cos30° = 78.4\times0.866 = 67.9$ N
Friction
$F = \mu R = 0.4\times67.9 = 27.2$ N (down slope)
Net force along slope (up +ve)
$F_{net} = 95 - mg\sin30° - F$
$= 95 - 8\times9.8\times0.5 - 27.2$
$= 95 - 39.2 - 27.2 = 28.6$ N
Acceleration
$a = \dfrac{F_{net}}{m} = \dfrac{28.6}{8} = 3.575$ m s$^{-2}$
✔ Answer
$a \approx \mathbf{3.58}$ m s$^{-2}$ up the slope
F7
A 3500 kg automobile travels down a 10° inclined road at 20 m s$^{-1}$. The driver jams the brakes, locking the wheels. The coefficient of friction between wheels and road is 0.5. Determine:
  1. The deceleration of the automobile after brakes are applied.
  2. The distance the tyres would skid on the road (to rest).
  3. The loss in kinetic energy if the car moves 5 m after brakes are applied.
💡 Hints
Forces on incline (going down)
Weight component DOWN slope: $mg\sin10°$. Friction acts UP slope (opposing motion): $F=\mu mg\cos10°$. Net force along slope = $mg\sin10° - \mu mg\cos10°$ (decelerating).
Note signs carefully
Car is going down. Friction opposes motion so acts up. Net deceleration $a = g(\mu\cos\theta - \sin\theta)$.
📋 Full Steps
Part (i) — Deceleration
$R = mg\cos10° = 3500\times9.8\times0.9848 = 33,730$ N
$F_{friction} = 0.5\times33730 = 16,865$ N (up slope)
$F_{gravity\parallel} = mg\sin10° = 3500\times9.8\times0.1736 = 5,958$ N (down slope)
Net force (taking down as +ve): $F_{net} = 5958 - 16865 = -10,907$ N (i.e., up slope = deceleration)
$a = \dfrac{10907}{3500} = 3.116$ m s$^{-2}$ (deceleration)
Part (ii) — Skid distance
$u=20$, $v=0$, $a=-3.116$: $v^2=u^2+2as$
$0 = 400 - 2(3.116)s$
$s = \dfrac{400}{6.232} = 64.2$ m
Part (iii) — KE loss over 5 m
$v^2 = 20^2 - 2(3.116)(5) = 400 - 31.16 = 368.84$
$\Delta KE = \tfrac{1}{2}m(u^2-v^2) = \tfrac{1}{2}(3500)(400-368.84) = 1750\times31.16 = 54{,}530$ J
✔ Answer
(i) Deceleration $\approx \mathbf{3.12}$ m s$^{-2}$
(ii) Skid distance $\approx \mathbf{64.2}$ m
(iii) KE loss over 5 m $\approx \mathbf{54{,}530}$ J $= 54.53$ kJ

K1
An elevator starts from rest with a constant acceleration of 2 m s$^{-2}$ until it attains a speed of 4 m s$^{-1}$. It then immediately decelerates at 3 m s$^{-2}$ until rest.
  1. Calculate the total time for the journey.
  2. Calculate the total distance travelled.
💡 Hints
Two phases
Phase 1: $u=0$, $v=4$, $a=2$. Phase 2: $u=4$, $v=0$, $a=-3$. Use $v=u+at$ to find each time, $s=\frac{1}{2}(u+v)t$ for each distance.
📋 Full Steps
Phase 1 (accelerating)
$v = u + at_1 \Rightarrow 4 = 0 + 2t_1 \Rightarrow t_1 = 2$ s
$s_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}(0+4)(2) = 4$ m
Phase 2 (decelerating)
$0 = 4 - 3t_2 \Rightarrow t_2 = \tfrac{4}{3}$ s
$s_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(4+0)\tfrac{4}{3} = \tfrac{8}{3}$ m
Totals
$t = 2 + \tfrac{4}{3} = \tfrac{10}{3} \approx 3.33$ s
$s = 4 + \tfrac{8}{3} = \tfrac{20}{3} \approx 6.67$ m
✔ Answer
(i) Total time $= \dfrac{10}{3} \approx \mathbf{3.33}$ s
(ii) Total distance $= \dfrac{20}{3} \approx \mathbf{6.67}$ m

A velocity–time graph shows a package falling from a plane with a parachute. Key values: rises from 0 to 50 m s$^{-1}$ from $t=0$ to $t=10$ s; falls from 50 to 15 m s$^{-1}$ from $t=10$ to $t=25$ s (parachute opens); remains at 15 m s$^{-1}$ from $t=25$ to $t=55$ s.

Area under v-t graph = distance

K4
A railway carriage (mass 700 kg) ascends a smooth plane inclined at 18° for 30 m. Speed at start = 18 m s$^{-1}$; speed at finish = 10 m s$^{-1}$.
  1. Calculate the deceleration of the carriage.
  2. Calculate the tractive force.
💡 Hints
Part (i)
Use $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ with $u=18$, $v=10$, $s=30$.
Part (ii)
Newton's 2nd along slope: $F_{tractive} - mg\sin18° = ma$. Smooth plane means no friction.

Deceleration
$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$
$100 = 324 + 2a(30)$

🚗 Newton's Laws & Connected Particles
$F=ma$ · Inclined planes · Rough surfaces · Atwood's machine · Pulley systems
N1
Car A (mass 1500 kg) is on a rough horizontal road connected by a cable over a smooth pulley at the edge of a cliff to Car B (mass 1000 kg) hanging freely. The driving force of Car A just prevents it from moving towards the pulley. Coefficient of friction $\mu = 0.6$.
  1. Determine the value of the driving force.
  2. If Car A's engine shuts off suddenly, calculate the acceleration of Car A.
💡 Hints
Part (i) — Equilibrium
System not moving. For Car B: $T = 1000g$. For Car A: $D + F_{friction} = T$ (driving force + friction from road both oppose the pull toward cliff). Wait — friction opposes TENDENCY of motion. Car A tends to move TOWARD pulley (pulled by T), so friction acts AWAY from pulley. Driving force also acts away from pulley. So: $D + F = T$.
Part (ii) — Engine off
Now: Tension $T$ pulls A toward cliff; friction still opposes motion (so away from cliff). Net force on system: $m_B g - \mu m_A g = (m_A + m_B)a$.
📋 Full Steps
Part (i)
Car B stationary: $T = m_B g = 1000\times9.8 = 9800$ N
Car A: friction $= \mu m_A g = 0.6\times1500\times9.8 = 8820$ N (away from pulley)
Equilibrium: $D + 8820 = 9800$
$D = 9800 - 8820 = 980$ N
Part (ii) — Engine off, system accelerates
Driving force gone. Net force (taking toward pulley as +ve):
$F_{net} = m_B g - \mu m_A g = 9800 - 8820 = 980$ N
Total mass $= 1500+1000 = 2500$ kg
$a = \dfrac{980}{2500} = 0.392$ m s$^{-2}$ (Car A accelerates toward pulley/cliff)
✔ Answer
(i) Driving force $D = \mathbf{980}$ N
(ii) Acceleration $= \mathbf{0.392}$ m s$^{-2}$
N2
Two particles of mass 12 kg and 8 kg are connected by a light inextensible string over a smooth pulley. Both start at rest, 100 m above the ground.
  1. Determine the acceleration of the system.
  2. Determine the tension in the string.
  3. Find the distance each particle travels after 6 seconds.
  4. Find the velocity of the 12 kg particle just before it hits the ground, if disconnected 6 seconds after release.
💡 Hints
Atwood's machine
$a = \dfrac{(m_1 - m_2)g}{m_1+m_2}$. Tension: $T = \dfrac{2m_1m_2g}{m_1+m_2}$.
After disconnection
At $t=6$ s, 12 kg has velocity $v = at$. After disconnect, 12 kg falls freely under gravity from that velocity.
📋 Full Steps
Part (i) — Acceleration
$a = \dfrac{(12-8)\times9.8}{12+8} = \dfrac{4\times9.8}{20} = \dfrac{39.2}{20} = 1.96$ m s$^{-2}$
Part (ii) — Tension
For 8 kg: $T - 8g = 8a \Rightarrow T = 8(9.8+1.96) = 8\times11.76 = 94.08$ N
Check with 12 kg: $12g - T = 12a \Rightarrow T = 12(9.8-1.96) = 12\times7.84 = 94.08$ ✓
Part (iii) — Distance in 6 s

∫ Differential Equations in Mechanics
Formulating $ma = F(v)$ · Separable ODEs · Variable resistance / force · Finding terminal velocity
ODE1
A vehicle of mass 1200 kg starts from rest on a horizontal road. Driving force = 800 N; resistance = 200$v$ N (proportional to velocity $v$).
  1. Derive a differential equation to model the motion.
  2. Hence show that $v = 4\!\left(1-e^{-t/6}\right)$.
  3. Determine the time taken for the vehicle to reach 3 m s$^{-1}$.
💡 Hints
Part (i) — Newton's 2nd
$ma = F_{net} = 800 - 200v$. So $1200\dfrac{dv}{dt} = 800-200v$.
Part (ii) — Separate variables
$\dfrac{dv}{4-v} = \dfrac{1}{6}dt$ (after simplifying). Integrate both sides. Apply IC $v=0$ at $t=0$.
Terminal velocity
As $t\to\infty$, $e^{-t/6}\to0$, so $v\to4$ m s$^{-1}$ (terminal velocity).
📋 Full Steps
Part (i) — ODE
$F_{net} = 800 - 200v$
$ma = 1200\dfrac{dv}{dt} = 800-200v$
$\dfrac{dv}{dt} = \dfrac{800-200v}{1200} = \dfrac{4-v}{6}$
Part (ii) — Solve by separation
$\dfrac{dv}{4-v} = \dfrac{dt}{6}$
$\int\dfrac{dv}{4-v} = \int\dfrac{dt}{6}$
$-\ln|4-v| = \dfrac{t}{6} + C$
At $t=0$, $v=0$: $-\ln4 = C$
$-\ln(4-v) = \dfrac{t}{6} - \ln4$
$\ln\dfrac{4}{4-v} = \dfrac{t}{6}$
$\dfrac{4}{4-v} = e^{t/6}$
$4-v = 4e^{-t/6}$
$v = 4(1-e^{-t/6})$ ✓
Part (iii) — Time for v = 3
$3 = 4(1-e^{-t/6})$
$\dfrac{3}{4} = 1-e^{-t/6}$
$e^{-t/6} = \dfrac{1}{4}$
$-\dfrac{t}{6} = \ln\dfrac{1}{4} = -\ln4$
$t = 6\ln4 = 6\times1.3863 = 8.318$ s
✔ Answer
(i) $1200\dfrac{dv}{dt} = 800-200v$
(iii) $t = 6\ln4 \approx \mathbf{8.32}$ s
Terminal velocity $= 4$ m s$^{-1}$
ODE2
A car (mass 1500 kg) has constant power engine and travels at constant speed. State the condition for constant speed, then:

A particle moving in a straight line has $a = v\dfrac{dv}{dx}$ where resistance is $kv^2$.

More directly: outline the principle of conservation of energy and the principle of conservation of linear momentum.
💡 Hints
Principles
These are definition questions — know the exact wording for CAPE exams.
✔ Answer
Conservation of Energy: The total energy of a closed system remains constant — energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another. (Mechanical form: $KE + PE = \text{constant}$ when no non-conservative forces act.)

Conservation of Linear Momentum: In a closed system where no external forces act, the total linear momentum before a collision (or interaction) is equal to the total linear momentum after.

Constant speed condition: Net force = 0, i.e., driving/tractive force = total resistance.
$F_{drive} = F_{resistance}$ (for constant speed)
$P = Fv$ (power = driving force × velocity)
